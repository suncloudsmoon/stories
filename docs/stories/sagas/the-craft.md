# The Craft — Why a Spine, Not a Template

**The hook.** A story must be a work of art *and* be produced reliably, on every code change, by a machine. Those two goals pull against each other — and how we resolved the tension is the whole craft.

**The world before.** Two failure modes loomed. A **rigid template** ("Problem / Solution / Notes") makes output consistent but kills the art — every story reads like a filled-in form. **Pure freeform** keeps the art but drifts: it skips the why, buries the lede, sprawls. Neither produces a soul on demand.

**The decision.** A **light spine**, not a template (`skills/writing-a-story/SKILL.md:21`): hook → the world before → the decision & why → what it means → links onward. A spine you *bend* — a vignette collapses it to a few lines, an origin saga dwells. Two guards keep it honest: the bar **scales by kind** (don't dress up a raw source), and the model must **read before it writes** — open the actual code and sources, write from knowledge, never a guess. A story invented from a hunch misleads with authority; that failure mode earned its own explicit rule.

**What it means.** Consistency comes from the spine and the quality bar, not from a fill-in-the-blanks shape — so the art survives. The load-bearing section is always *the decision & why*, cited with `path:line`. If a story drifts into restating what the code obviously does, it has failed the bar and should be cut, not padded. Hold the line: tight beats long, grounded beats clever.
